MODULE 3 - Networking Media

Terms Definition
Coaxial Cable Consists of a core of copper wire surrounded by insulation, a braided metal shielding, and an outer cover.
Shielding Refers to the woven or stranded metal mesh that surrounds some types of cabling.
Noise Stray electronic signals
Dual Shielded Cable that contains one layer of foil insulation and one layer of braided metal shielding
Crosstalk Is signal overflow from an adjacent wire
Electrical short Occurs when any two conducting wires or a conducting wire and a ground come into contact with each other.
Attenuation Is the loss of signal strength that begins to occur as the signal travels farther along a copper cable.
Thinnet cable Is a flexible coaxial cable about 0.64 centimeters (0.25 inches) thick.
Thicknet cable Bus Is a relatively rigid coaxial cable about 1.27 centimeters (0.5 inches) in diameter.
BNC T Connector This connector joins the network interface card (NIC) in the computer to the network cable.
BNC Barrel Connector This connector is used to join two lengths of thinnet cable to make one longer length.
BNC Terminator Closes each end of the bus cable to absorb stray signals.
Twisted-pair cable Consists of two insulated strands of copper wire twisted around each other.
Unshielded Twisted Pair Is the most popular type of twisted-pair cable and is fast becoming the most popular LAN cabling.
Category 1 (UTP) Refers to traditional UTP telephone cable that can carry voice but not data transmissions.
Category 2 (UTP) Certifies UTP cable for data transmissions up to 4 megabits per second (Mbps). It consists of four twisted pairs of copper wire.
Category 3 (UTP) Certifies UTP cable for data transmissions up to 16 Mbps. It consists of four twisted pairs of copper wire with three twists per foot.
Category 4 (UTP) Certifies UTP cable for data transmissions up to 20 Mbps. It consists of four twisted pairs of copper wire.
Category 5 (UTP) Certifies UTP cable for data transmissions up to 100 Mbps. It consists of four twisted pairs of copper wire.
Category 6 (UTP) up to 1000 Mbps (200 MHz)
Shielded Twisted Pair Uses a woven copper-braid jacket that is more protective and of a higher quality than the jacket used by UTP
RJ-45 Is slightly larger and will not fit into the RJ-11 telephone jack. Houses eight cable connections.
Distribution racks and rack sleeve Can create more room for cables where there isn't much floor space.
Expandable patch panels These come in various versions that support up to 96 ports and transmission speeds of up to 100 Mbps.
Jack couplers These single or double RJ-45 jacks snap into patch panels and wall plates and support data rates of up to 100 Mbps.
Wall plates These support two or more couplers.
Fiber optic cable Carry digital data signals in the form of modulated pulses of light.
Core Extremely thin cylinder of glass.
Cladding A concentric layer of glass.
Bandwidth Refers to the measure of the capacity of a medium to transmit data.
Baseband system Use digital signaling over a single channel.
Broadband system Use analog signaling and a range of frequencies.
Work Area Is where computer workstations and other user devices are located.
Horizontal Wiring Runs from the work areaâ€™s wall jack to the telecommunications closet and is usually terminated at a patch panel.
Equipment Room Houses servers, routers, switches, and other major network equipment, and serves as a connection point for backbone cabling running between TCs.
Backbone Cabling Interconnects TCs and equipment rooms.
Entrance Facility Is the location of the cabling and equipment that connects a corporate network to a third-party telecommunications provider.
Access Point Device Includes an antenna and a transmitter to send and receive wireless traffic, but also connects to the wired side of the network
Transceiver Translate between the wired and wireless networks
802.11 wireless Is an extension to Ethernet using airwaves as the medium.
802.16 Worldwide Interoperability for Microwave Access (WiMax) - promise wireless broadband to outlying and rural areas, where last-mile wired connections are too expensive or impractical because of rough terrain.
Mobile WiMax: 802.16e Promises to allow users to roam from area to area without losing the connection, which offers mobility much like cell phone users enjoy.
